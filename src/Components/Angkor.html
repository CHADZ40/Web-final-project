Nestled deep within the lush landscapes of Siem Reap, Cambodia, stands Angkor Wat—the largest religious monument in the world and an architectural masterpiece that bridges the gap between the earthly and the divine. Originally constructed in the early 12th century by King Suryavarman II, this breathtaking complex was initially dedicated to the Hindu god Vishnu before gradually transforming into a Buddhist temple site towards the end of the century. Today, it remains the ultimate symbol of Cambodian soul, pride, and heritage, proudly emblazoned on the country’s national flag.

Food and Culture

Food

Fish Amok

Fish amok is widely considered the crown jewel of Cambodian cuisine, with its roots deeply tied to the ancient Khmer Empire centered in Siem Reap. The local freshwater fish caught from the Tonle Sap lake is steamed to perfection with a rich, fragrant kroeung curry paste and coconut milk.

Num Banhchok

Often referred to as "Khmer rice noodles," this is a popular morning or afternoon dish. It consists of fresh, lightly fermented rice noodles topped with a cold, fish-based green curry gravy made from lemongrass, fingerroot ginger, and kaffir lime leaves, piled high with fresh greens.

Culture

Apsara

The Apsara represents the ultimate symbol of Khmer feminine grace, bridging the earthly realm with the divine through sacred movement. If you look closely at the walls of Angkor Wat, you can see how these celestial dancers have watched over the temples for centuries.

Explore More

Temple

The temples of Siem Reap stand as magnificent stone monuments of the ancient Khmer Empire, featuring towering spires and walls carved with intricate celestial dancers. They remain active sacred sites where the scent of incense and the presence of monks bring centuries of spiritual heritage to life. Seeing these grand structures catch the morning light and reflect across the surrounding water is a breathtaking experience.

Angkor Wat — General Day Flow

Time of Day What to Do
Early Morning Head out while it's still dark to catch sunrise at the temple. This is the most popular time to visit, so expect company at the main viewing spots.
Sunrise Watch the sky change color over the temple towers — the classic Angkor Wat moment most travelers plan their whole day around.
Morning Explore Angkor Wat itself while it's cool and the light is soft. A good time to slow down and take in the carvings and courtyards before the heat picks up.
Breakfast Grab something simple nearby — this is when locals often eat noodle soup, a good moment to introduce that as part of the food experience rather than skipping straight to lunch.
Mid-Morning Move on to nearby temples, still relatively cool, and a good contrast to Angkor Wat's style.
Late Morning Visit a temple known for its atmosphere, where nature has grown into the ruins — a favorite for photos.
Midday Take a lunch break as the heat peaks. A great time to try a signature Cambodian dish and rest before continuing.
Afternoon Optional downtime, or continue exploring quieter, less-visited temples if energy allows.
Late Afternoon Head to a hilltop or elevated spot for sunset views over the temple complex — a nice bookend to the sunrise start.
Evening Wind down with dinner and, if timing works, a cultural dance performance that reflects the same stories seen carved into the temples earlier that day.
